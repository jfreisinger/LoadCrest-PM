# SK-004: Group Flow Facilitation

## Purpose

Provides a structured method for designing and running in-person collaborative sessions — team working sessions, partner engagements, customer discovery, and cross-disciplinary workshops — that reliably generate Group Flow: the state of collective intelligence where the group produces insights no individual could reach alone. Grounded in Positive Psychology and Keith Sawyer's research on collaborative emergence.

Read [culture/GROUP-FLOW.md](../culture/GROUP-FLOW.md) first to understand the philosophy behind this skill.

---

## When to Use

- Before any in-person session where the outcome depends on genuine creative or analytical collaboration (not just information transfer)
- When designing partner working sessions with Dyna Power, IONATE, VEIR, Sandia, or USAF
- When a team working session has been unproductive or left people feeling depleted rather than energized
- When facing a genuinely novel problem where no single person has the answer
- When preparing for a high-stakes customer discovery or investor meeting

## Steps

### Phase 1: Design (Before the Session)

1. **Write the single goal** — Articulate exactly what the group will have produced by the end. Not "discuss the architecture" but "agree on the interface spec between Sentinel and the Dyna Power converter." Ambiguous goals prevent flow.

2. **Size and balance the room** — Group Flow research shows 4–7 participants is optimal. Beyond 8, equal participation collapses. If more people must be present, split into parallel working groups with a synthesis phase.

3. **Surface common interests** — Before the session, identify at least one area of genuine shared interest between participants beyond the transactional agenda. This is the seed of familiarity that Group Flow requires. For partner sessions, this may come from a pre-call or LinkedIn/professional review.

4. **Build shared context** — Send a one-page pre-read no more than 48 hours before. It should frame the problem, not propose a solution. A proposed solution closes down generative thinking before the room assembles.

5. **Design the physical space** — See Environment Design section below.

6. **Assign a facilitator** — The facilitator does not contribute content during flow phases. Their job is to hold the conditions. Rotate this role across team members over time.

---

### Phase 2: Open (First 15–20 Minutes)

7. **Connect before content** — Begin with a brief personal check-in: one sentence each on where people are arriving from (mentally, emotionally, logistically). This is not small talk — it activates listening and surfaces who needs more space in the room today.

8. **State the shared interest** — Explicitly name one thing everyone in the room cares about beyond today's agenda. For a partner session: *"Before we get into the spec, I want to name that everyone in this room wants to see 800VDC succeed at scale — that's the shared ground we're working from."* This activates Sawyer's *blending egos* condition.

9. **Frame the goal and constraints** — State the session goal (from Step 1), the time available, and any hard constraints (decisions that can't be unmade, things outside scope). Write the goal visibly on a whiteboard or shared surface.

10. **Establish the operating mode** — Name the working style explicitly: *"In this session, we build on ideas before we evaluate them. Incomplete thoughts are welcome — speak them aloud anyway."* This activates the *yes, and* condition.

---

### Phase 3: Work (The Flow Session)

11. **Protect concentration** — Phones silent and face-down. No laptops unless they're the working surface. One conversation at a time. The facilitator enforces this without apology.

12. **Equalize voice** — Track who's speaking. If one person has spoken three times and another hasn't spoken, the facilitator redirects: *"[Name], you've been quiet — what's your read on this?"* Do this early; it becomes harder as the session progresses.

13. **Build, don't evaluate mid-flight** — When an idea surfaces, the next two contributions must *extend* it (add to, complicate, apply, make concrete) before anyone evaluates it. The facilitator interrupts premature evaluation: *"Hold that critique — let's build this out another two steps first."*

14. **Make thinking visible** — Write everything on the shared surface. Diagrams, fragments, questions, half-formed models. The visual record keeps the group's working memory synchronized and prevents good ideas from evaporating.

15. **Name the energy** — When the group hits a flat spot, name it: *"We seem to have run into a wall. Let's step back — what assumption are we all making that might be wrong?"* This resets concentration without blame.

16. **Use the challenge signal** — Group Flow requires the problem to be hard enough to matter. If the session feels too easy, the facilitator escalates: *"If this solution works, what's the failure mode we haven't discussed?"*

---

### Phase 4: Close (Final 20–30 Minutes)

17. **Synthesize before you leave the room** — With 20 minutes remaining, shift from generation to synthesis. The facilitator draws a boundary: *"We have 20 minutes. Let's consolidate what we've agreed on."* Do not let the session end in a field of open threads.

18. **Document the shared artifact** — Every session produces one shared artifact before participants leave:
    - A decision (stated precisely, written down, owned by a name)
    - A diagram (photographed and saved)
    - A priority list (ranked, with owners)
    - A next-meeting input (the single open question that will drive the next session)

19. **Name what was good** — Positive Psychology research consistently shows that closing with explicit recognition of what worked — *in the session*, not just the output — reinforces the behaviors that created it. Spend 3 minutes: each person names one thing the group did well today.

20. **Assign the follow-through** — One person owns distributing the artifact within 24 hours. No artifact = the session didn't happen.

## Environment Design

The physical environment is not decorative — it is a flow condition. Use this checklist:

**Space**
- [ ] Walls or surfaces for writing (whiteboard, windows + markers, butcher paper)
- [ ] Seating that allows eye contact between all participants (round or U-shape, not theater rows)
- [ ] No screen at the focal point — screens encourage presentation mode, not conversation mode
- [ ] Natural light if possible; avoid fluorescent overhead lighting

**Objects**
- [ ] Physical markers + erasers (low-friction writing)
- [ ] Sticky notes for idea capture in parallel
- [ ] A clock visible to the facilitator only (not to the group — removes clock-watching)
- [ ] Water and light food available without interruption

**Logistics**
- [ ] Do not schedule back-to-back — Group Flow requires a decompression buffer
- [ ] Start on time, every time — this signals that presence is valued
- [ ] No external observers unless they are actively participating

## Facilitation Anti-Patterns to Avoid

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Kills Flow |
|--------------|------------------|
| Starting with status updates | Orients people backward; wastes generative energy |
| Presenting a fully-formed solution | Closes the problem space before the group can open it |
| Allowing one voice to dominate | Breaks equal participation; others disengage |
| Evaluating ideas as they emerge | Activates social caution; people stop speaking half-formed thoughts |
| Letting the session end without a shared artifact | Leaves participants with no signal that presence mattered |
| Defaulting to video when in-person is feasible | Eliminates non-verbal channel; reduces familiarity accumulation |

## Adapting for Partner Sessions

Partner sessions (Dyna Power, IONATE, VEIR, Sandia, USAF) carry additional dynamics:

- **Expertise asymmetry is a feature, not a problem** — design a phase where each party teaches the other something. This builds familiarity fast and activates equal participation.
- **Shared mission framing** — open with the shared interest (the future of 800VDC, the reliability of AI infrastructure, the climate) before getting into the commercial or technical agenda.
- **One-pager pre-alignment** — send a one-page frame 48 hours before. For partners with institutional timelines (Sandia, USAF), this is especially important.
- **Joint artifact ownership** — when the session produces a shared document, both parties should have their name on it. This encodes the collaboration and creates continuity.

---

## Adapting for Customer Discovery

- **Listen-first mode** — the first 40% of the session is pure listening. Resist the urge to present. Use open questions: *"Walk me through a day when the power situation was especially frustrating."*
- **Reflect before proposing** — before offering any LoadCrest framing, reflect back what you heard: *"It sounds like the core issue is X. Is that right?"* Group Flow in discovery means the customer reaches their own insight with you present, not that you convince them of yours.
- **No slides in the listening phase** — slides signal that you've already made up your mind.

## Notes & Tips

- The single most powerful intervention a facilitator can make is **early equalization of voice**. Do it in the first 15 minutes or it becomes structurally difficult.
- Group Flow cannot be forced. It can only be invited. The facilitator's job is to remove obstacles, not manufacture enthusiasm.
- Sessions that feel slightly uncomfortable — where people are unsure of the answer — are closer to flow conditions than sessions that feel smooth and efficient. Discomfort means the problem is real.
- Build facilitator skill deliberately. Debrief after every session you facilitate: what condition was hardest to hold? What would you do differently?
